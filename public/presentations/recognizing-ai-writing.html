Recognizing AI Writing

Stephen Zweibel

Digital Scholarship Librarian, Graduate Center, CUNY

AI detectors don't work very well

  • False positives are common, including on real student writing
  • Anyone who edits AI output a little can defeat them
  • Pangram and the others give you a probability score, not a verdict
  • I find detection a losing place to start

Mostly, it's exposure

  • Read enough AI writing and you start to see it
  • No single tell does the work
  • Here are a few things to look for

Read for the template

  • Ask what rhetorical pattern the text is running
  • Each paragraph in AI prose tends to do a specific rhetorical job
  • The opener often announces what that job is
  • The reader has something to point at

The first-sentence scan

  • Read only the first sentence of each paragraph before reading for content
  • If those openers form a neat rhetorical ladder, the writing may be running an AI template
  • It's the fastest method I know that doesn't need a tool

Lowercase and “lowkey”

  • People humanize AI output by going lowercase, sprinkling in “lowkey,” breaking into fragments
  • The surface looks informal
  • The paragraph structure underneath is unchanged
  • The first-sentence scan still catches it

Modern Love (NYT, November 2025)

Kate Gilgan, “I Was Deemed Unfit to Be a Mother.” Readers said it sounded like AI. Gilgan later said she ran the essay through ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, and Perplexity for feedback on structure and tone, but did not paste generated text in.

Not hate. Not anger. Just the flat finality of a heart too tired to keep trying. That's when I stopped fighting. I didn't give up.

The line that triggered the suspicion. Short fragments arranged for impact, a metaphor that explains the feeling too neatly, and a closing turn that reads like an essay outline rather than a memory.

Ten of the fifteen books on the syndicated summer reading list don't exist. They were attributed to real authors with plausible-sounding descriptions.

The descriptions read fine. The books just aren't real. Min Jin Lee posted on X: “I have not written and will not be writing a novel called ‘Nightshade Market.’”

Gannett ran AI-written recaps of high school games. Readers ridiculed them. Gannett paused the experiment.

Former NBA participant Brandon Hunter, who beforehand performed for the Boston Celtics and Orlando Magic, has handed away on the age of 42, as introduced by Ohio males's basketball coach Jeff Boals on Tuesday… he performed in 67 video games over two seasons and achieved a career-high of 17 factors in a recreation in opposition to the Milwaukee Bucks in 2004.

The whole piece reads like a bad translation. “Performed” for played, “factors” for points, “handed away” for passed away. A product blurb survives this kind of error; an obituary doesn't.

Hachette's Shy Girl (March 2026)

Mia Ballard's horror novel, canceled in the U.S. and discontinued in the U.K. after readers flagged suspected AI prose. A passage from the book:

My breath evens out, the outline of possibility taking shape. A job. The thought lingers, solid and improbable. A flicker of hope. When the call ends, I sit for a moment, the phone warm in my hand, the silence loud. That fragile swell of hope trying to push through the cracks. But the feeling doesn't last. The buzz of a notification snaps me back, its vibration rippling through the table like an electric jolt. Nathan's name flashes on the screen. My chest tightens, anticipation blooming sharp and fast, and I unlock the phone with a clumsiness that betrays me.

Overuse of parallelism. Endless repetition of the same vocabulary. Awkward and abundant similes. Ballard says a freelance editor added AI-generated elements without her knowledge.

The AI apology formula

After Fortnite pro Peterbot was caught using racial slurs, his apology read like every AI-generated apology of the last two years.

I recognize now how deeply wrong and hurtful that language is. I take full responsibility for saying it, regardless of my age or the pressure I felt in that moment. I want to sincerely apologize to the communities affected and to anyone who has been hurt by my words. I am committed to continuing to learn, grow, and ensure that my actions moving forward reflect respect and understanding.

The shape: context, responsibility, apology, growth. The same template runs across corporate apologies, athlete apologies, and CEO statements.

LA Times “Insights” (2025)

The LA Times attached AI-generated counterpoints to opinion pieces. On Gustavo Arellano's column about Anaheim's KKK city council, one bullet point read:

Local historical accounts occasionally frame the 1920s Klan as a product of ‘white Protestant culture’ responding to societal changes rather than an explicitly hate-driven movement, minimizing its ideological threat.

On Jackie Calmes's piece comparing Trump to Nazi Germany, the AI Insights bullet was:

Trump's actions, while norm-breaking, lack the systemic violence and ideological coherence of Hitler's regime.

Both-sides language replacing argument. The tool was pulled off the Anaheim piece after the KKK counterpoint went public.

Vanity Fair: “Dario Amodei Has a Cold”

Joe Hagan disclosed his method in the article itself:

Dario Amodei never actually gave me an interview, stiffing me after months of planning. So I created a version of ‘Dario Amodei’ using his own machine. I fed Claude several published interviews, including everything Amodei said at Davos, plus the contents of his two books of essays, and told Claude to simulate the interview using variations on real quotes, and to make it like a scene from Raymond Chandler's The Big Sleep. It took Claude less than three minutes.

Disclosed and deliberate. The opposite end of the spectrum from a hidden AI byline. Useful to show alongside the failure cases.

Authorship is a spectrum

Pangram now describes four points along a spectrum.

  • Fully human-written
  • Lightly AI-assisted
  • Moderately AI-assisted
  • Fully AI-generated

Many of the cases readers flag fall in the middle two. Disclosure policy hasn't caught up to that yet.

Exercise

Hand out three short pieces with the sources hidden:

  • An unedited AI response
  • An AI response edited for tone but not structure
  • A human-written piece with its own unevenness and local specificity

Ask people to mark:

  • Paragraph openers that announce the paragraph's job
  • Phrases that announce significance without backing it up
  • Places where a concrete detail would sharpen the prose

The goal

  • Read more carefully
  • Revise more carefully
  • Ask better questions about authorship and disclosure
  • Catching cheaters is a different problem, and the detectors don't work anyway
