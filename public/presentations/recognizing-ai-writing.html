Recognizing AI Writing

Stephen Zweibel

Digital Scholarship Librarian, Graduate Center, CUNY

AI detectors don't work very well

  • False positives are common, including on real student writing
  • Anyone who edits AI output a little can defeat them
  • Pangram and the others give you a probability score, not a verdict
  • I find detection a losing place to start

Mostly, it's exposure

  • Read enough AI writing and you start to see it
  • No single tell does the work
  • Here are a few things to look for

Read for the template

  • Ask what rhetorical pattern the text is running
  • Each paragraph in AI prose tends to do a specific rhetorical job
  • The opener often announces what that job is
  • The reader has something to point at

The first-sentence scan

  • Read only the first sentence of each paragraph before reading for content
  • If those openers form a neat rhetorical ladder, the writing may be running an AI template
  • It's the fastest method I know that doesn't need a tool

Try the first-sentence scan

Read only the first sentence of each paragraph. The shape comes through.

Lowercase and “lowkey”

  • People humanize AI output by going lowercase, sprinkling in “lowkey,” breaking into fragments
  • The surface looks informal
  • The paragraph structure underneath is unchanged
  • The first-sentence scan still catches it

Modern Love (NYT, 2026)

Kate Gilgan, “Modern Love” column. Readers said it sounded like AI. The author later said she used AI tools for “inspiration and guidance and correction.”

Not hate. Not anger. Just the flat finality of a heart too tired to keep trying.

Short fragments arranged for impact, a metaphor that explains the feeling too neatly. No actual heartbreak sounds like this.

Vanity Fair: Ye email Q&A (2026)

After Ye's WSJ apology advertisement, Vanity Fair published written answers attributed to him. Roger Friedman at Showbiz411 wrote that the email interview “sure sounds like AI answered the questions.”

  • Heavy PR polish in the phrasing
  • Vague apology vocabulary: “remorse,” “treatment,” “meaningful change,” “positive course”
  • One medication answer folds in stats that read as sourced
  • The article format leaves the actual writer unnamed

Margaux Blanchard (Wired, BI, others, 2025)

Wired and Business Insider removed pieces by “Margaux Blanchard,” a freelancer who appears not to exist. The whole package was constructed: pitch, byline, bio, personal anecdotes, verification trail.

archivists of truth
exists in the industry's collective memory

Cinematic occupational language. None of it refers to anything real.

Sports Illustrated (2023)

SI published commerce content under fake author names with AI-generated headshots and bios. The reviews themselves had the tell.

volleyball “can be a little tricky to get into, especially without an actual ball”

The prose states something so basic it sounds alien. The system seems to have learned about volleyball from a database row.

Chicago Sun-Times reading list (May 2025)

A syndicated summer guide ran in the Sun-Times and the Philadelphia Inquirer with fake book recommendations attributed to real authors.

  • Nightshade Market by Min Jin Lee
  • The Longest Day by Rumaan Alam
  • The Rainmakers by Percival Everett

None of these books exist. The check is just looking them up.

CNET Money explainers (2023)

CNET ran AI-assisted finance pieces under the “CNET Money Staff” byline, then had to correct dozens of them.

The choice between a bank and credit union is not one-size-fits-all.

The prose is competent. It would fit in any consumer-finance explainer. Fluent generic prose is the hardest kind to catch.

Gannett high school sports recaps (2023)

Gannett ran AI-written recaps of high school games. Readers ridiculed them. Gannett paused the experiment.

a close encounter of the athletic kind

The phrase showed up across multiple writeups. The recap had no player names and no reported scenes. The system was turning box scores into stock phrases.

MSN obituary for Brandon Hunter (2023)

MSN published an AI-generated obituary for the former NBA player Brandon Hunter, who died at 42.

  • headline: “Brandon Hunter useless at 42”
  • “performed in 67 video games”
  • “handed away”

Wrong word choices. The texture of bad translation. A product blurb survives this kind of error; an obituary doesn't.

Hachette's Shy Girl (March 2026)

Hachette canceled the U.S. publication of Mia Ballard's horror novel and discontinued the U.K. edition after AI-use concerns. Readers and reviewers flagged the book before the publisher acted.

  • This case shows tells at the level of the whole novel, beyond the sentence
  • AI fiction tends to over-explain themes
  • It also tends toward tidy, single-track plots with little temporal complexity
  • Hachette acted only after public pressure caught up to what readers had already noticed

The AI apology formula

After Fortnite pro Peterbot was caught making racist remarks, his apology read like every AI-generated apology of the last two years.

I want to sincerely apologize to the communities affected and to anyone who has been hurt by my words.

The shape: context, responsibility, apology, growth. The same template runs across corporate apologies, athlete apologies, and CEO statements.

LA Times “Insights” (2025)

The LA Times added an AI tool that attaches generated summaries and counterpoints to opinion pieces. The paper discloses the feature; the voice is the lesson.

  • The original opinion piece has a clear human stance
  • The AI counterpoint smooths it into both-sides language
  • Show students both, side by side
  • The tone teaches what disclosed AI writing sounds like

Vanity Fair: “Dario Amodei Has a Cold”

Joe Hagan didn't get an interview with the Anthropic CEO. So he fed Claude prior interviews, the Davos comments, and Amodei's essays, then asked Claude to simulate an interview in the style of Raymond Chandler's The Big Sleep. The simulation is in the article, disclosed.

  • Disclosed and deliberate
  • Where does the disclosure appear, and is it loud enough?
  • What does the simulation accomplish that a regular feature couldn't?
  • A useful one to show alongside the failure cases

Pangram's cafe-review contrast

AI

a delightful gem
inviting ambiance
the perfect place to unwind

HUMAN

some bites were just sausage and nothing else

The AI review performs satisfaction with the idea of a cafe. The human review has odd, specific details that come from an actual meal. Local friction is the thing AI prose has the hardest time faking.

Authorship is a spectrum

Pangram now describes four points along a spectrum.

  • Fully human-written
  • Lightly AI-assisted
  • Moderately AI-assisted
  • Fully AI-generated

Many of the cases readers flag fall in the middle two. Disclosure policy hasn't caught up to that yet.

Exercise

Hand out three short pieces with the sources hidden:

  • An unedited AI response
  • An AI response edited for tone but not structure
  • A human-written piece with its own unevenness and local specificity

Ask people to mark:

  • Paragraph openers that announce the paragraph's job
  • Phrases that announce significance without backing it up
  • Places where a concrete detail would sharpen the prose

The goal

  • Read more carefully
  • Revise more carefully
  • Ask better questions about authorship and disclosure
  • Catching cheaters is a different problem, and the detectors don't work anyway
